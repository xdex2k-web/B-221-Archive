Biases
Introduction
In this chapter, we continue our discussion of common stumbling blocks to good thinking, focusing specifically on cognitive biases. As we said before, these are shortcuts in reasoning that are useful in one way or another, but that systematically lead us astray when applied in inappropriate contexts.
It’s important to avoid confusing cognitive biases with the more common use of the term bias. We often use that term to signify a person’s prejudicial negative (or sometimes positive) attitudes towards an individual, or a tendency to engage in discrimination against some identifiable group. Racists, for example, are biased against people of a different ethnicity. Or we might worry that a professor who teaches their own children might be biased in their favour when it comes to assigning grades. This isn’t the sense of the word bias that’s at work when we talk about cognitive biases, so when we say that someone is acting under the influence of a cognitive bias, we’re not automatically saying that they’re doing something morally objectionable.
In the most basic sense of the word, a bias is a tendency or a leaning in a particular direction. Some biases are purely mechanical. A scale, for example, might have a bias in that it regularly says you weigh a few kilos more than you actually weigh. Such a scale still works—it’s merely inaccurate to a certain degree. Importantly, if you know that the scale you’re weighing yourself on is biased this way, you can correct for the bias by subtracting the right amount from the weight it tells you. But the scale is flawed, so anyone stuck using it must use some system to compensate for that.
A cognitive bias is a tilt or leaning, too, but a tilt or leaning in your mental processes, a tendency towards certain perceptions or conclusions. Like the flawed scale, your mind tends to produce certain incorrect results. But, as with the scale, if you know your mind has these biases, you can guard against them and work to overcome them
Why are there Cognitive Biases?
At a high level of abstraction, we can identify three features of our psychology that, though we need them, get us into trouble: we try to think fast, we try to save effort, and we want to feel good about ourselves. These are all essential for getting through the day. Consider our need to think fast: we simply don’t have time to carefully think through every little question or decision. Imagine just trying to figure out the best breakfast to have if you thought through every little detail. You’d need to consider the ingredients available to you, and the large number of possible meals you could make with those ingredients. You’d need to take into account the nutritional value of the various options, as well as their cost and how much enjoyment they’d bring you; maybe you’d also consider the environmental and social impact of your choice. And so on. You’d never get to actually eating, much less get past breakfast and go to work. The same goes for saving effort.

Thinking carefully requires effort, and we have limited resources. If we exhaust ourselves trying to get every little thing exactly right, we won’t have anything left in the tank when it really counts. Perhaps less obvious is the fact that feeling good about yourself is essential. But it’s true—without a certain level of self-confidence and optimism, you wouldn’t be able to tackle the small and large challenges life constantly throws at you. It’s essential that you’re able to get up in the morning, look in the mirror, and say to yourself Yes, I can do this. That’s part of the reason why chronically depressed people, in addition to feeling bad, often have serious trouble functioning.
However, our need to be self-confident and optimistic can easily lead to overconfidence and, more specifically, to patterns of ignoring potential problems and stumbling blocks. Similarly, our general desire for speed and effortlessness has an unfortunate side effect: we too easily accept intuitive answers to questions our intuition isn’t capable of handling well. The same desire also explains what’s sometimes referred to as the principle of substitution, which is one of the most insidious features of our psychology. The principle of substitution says that when we’re considering a difficult question for which our intuitive system 1 has no quick, intuitive answer, we subconsciously swap (substitute) the difficult question out and find a related question that system 1 can easily answer. For instance, if asked whether it’s wise to invest in a company’s stock, you’d have to first answer whether the company is currently valued appropriately and whether it’s likely to increase in value. Those are very difficult questions. Thus, it’d be tempting to focus instead on factors that are related but not quite relevant, such as whether the company’s stock has performed well in the past, or whether it has a popular brand name, or whether you liked their most recent TV commercial. One well-known example of substitution occurs when psychologists ask people how satisfied they are with their lives. That’s a difficult question to answer because you need to figure out what your standards are for what a good life is, and how your life measures up to them in various ways. Instead of doing all that work, people tend to resort to substitution by instead answering a much easier question: What is your current mood? We know this happens because people report much higher life-satisfaction under the influence of events that, while mood-affecting, are too trivial to make a difference to a reasonable evaluation of life as a whole (such as finding a small amount of money on the way to the interview, or walking there through good rather than bad weather). Substitution lies at the heart of many cognitive biases.
Before moving on to discussing some important specific cognitive biases, it’s worth repeating a point made in the first class of the course: knowing about these errors is often not enough to avoid them. We’re talking about powerful mental tendencies we can’t simply reason our way out of. Knowing about biases is an important first step towards identifying them in ourselves and others. But noticing them is often not enough to ensure we avoid them. It’s not that easy. To understand this, it’s helpful to think about these tendencies as analogous to powerful optical illusions, such as the Muller-Lyer illusion, depicted in figure 1.

Figure 1: The Muller-Lyer Illusion
As you can easily check with a ruler, the two lines in figure 1 are of identical length. Yet even when you know this, you almost certainly continue to see the line at the bottom as longer than the line at the top. You can know that your eyes (or rather your brain) deceives you in this instance, but this knowledge has no effect on what you see. So the strategy for dealing with the Muller-Lyer illusion isn’t to better train your eyes or look harder. Rather, you should simply not trust your eyes in situations where you need to know how long arrowed lines are, and instead put in the extra effort to measure them. The same is true for dealing with cognitive biases. It’s a bad idea to just try hard to avoid them. Instead, you need to put procedures into place that help you (and your team) get things right despite the presence of cognitive biases. We’ll return to this point later in this chapter.
Over the last few decades, psychologists have identified and studied a large number of cognitive biases. These have been documented and verified in hundreds of experiments, and are known to affect the reasoning of experts as well as the general population. We’ll discuss just a few of them, and their relevance to critical thinking in business decision-making.
Anchoring
Anchoring is the tendency to attach too much importance to a single piece of information acquired early in the decision-making process. Our brains have a tendency to hold onto bits of information presented to us and to let them subconsciously affect the way we look at subsequently presented information. As a result, claims we encounter early on can affect the decisions we make, even when our rational minds know them to be unimportant.
A classic example of anchoring occurs when people are asked to estimate, quickly and without a calculator, what 6 × 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 is. Most of us aren’t good at making snap judgements about math, so the estimates tend to be pretty far off. More important for our purposes is the fact that when people are instead asked to guess what 1 × 2 × 3 × 4 × 5 × 6 is, they give a much lower estimate. Mathematically, the two answers must be exactly the same: the order in which you multiply numbers doesn’t change the answer. But, as research has shown, people tend to be influenced by the size of the first number they hear, so people given a series of numbers starting with 6 tend to give a much higher estimate than people given the same series starting with 1.

In another example of anchoring, researchers have shown that people are unduly influenced by starting a conversation with a particular number, even when they know that number is effectively random. Researchers might ask participants, for instance, “What day of the month is your birthday? Think of that number and focus on that date. Now, how much would you pay for this bottle of wine?” How is that relevant? It’s not. But research shows that, in experiments like this, people born in the last part of the month tend to be willing to pay more than people born in the first part of the month—apparently, their judgement is influenced by the size of the number they were focusing on, though that obviously has absolutely nothing to do with the value of the bottle of wine in front of them.
This anchoring tendency can have important implications in the world of business. Imagine during a negotiation over price the other party opens discussion with a ridiculously low offer. You will of course reject the offer, but your counteroffer will likely be lower than it would have been had the opening offer been higher. Your mind is subconsciously pulled towards the lower number. As a result of this opening bid, you are essentially “locked in,” mentally, subconsciously treating the initial bid as a reference point, rather than thinking of what the deal is really worth to you. Your thinking, in other words, has been anchored by facts that may not be objectively important.
Framing Effects
A framing effect occurs when people who are given the same information can arrive at significantly different conclusions, depending on how exactly the information is worded, or framed. This is problematic because it leaves people open to being manipulated by a speaker’s choice of wording or framing, when all that should matter is the underlying reality being described.
Probably the most straightforward framing effect occurs when a project’s chances are described by giving either its likelihood of failure or its likelihood of success. People are much more likely, for example, to agree to a surgery when they’re told it has a 90% chance of success than if told it has a 10% chance of failure. Even though these two pieces of information describe identical odds, it appears that the mention of failure causes people to be a bit more nervous. This example is an instance of a family of framing effects revolving around the fact that people react badly to being explicitly confronted with bad outcomes or possibilities.
Imagine you've conducted a customer satisfaction survey and received approximately 200 responses. About 4% of the responses are negative. Would you rather tell your boss that you’ve discovered a 96% satisfaction rate or that you’ve discovered that there are eight very dissatisfied customers out there? The way you frame the data may affect the way your boss responds.

A different framing effect that can be important in business contexts has been demonstrated with participants in experiments involving some classic games of game theory. When participants were told the game they were playing was called The Wall Street Game, most of them chose strategies that were egoistic, focusing exclusively on profit for themselves. Another group of participants played the exact same game but was told the game was called The Cooperation Game; those participants chose much more cooperative strategies, trying to find ways for everyone to benefit. Rationally, the mere name of the game shouldn’t make any difference in how you play it, but it turns out that’s not how our minds work. Frames are powerful, and names can be frames.
Similarly, there are certain words that trigger a strong desire to avoid the associated outcomes. For instance, people are more motivated to avoid a loss than an identical cost (perhaps because people subconsciously think they must be getting something in return for that cost, even when that’s not the case). It’s also easier to motivate people to pay at an earlier time by threatening them with a late penalty as opposed to offering an early bird rebate.
Confirmation Bias
Confirmation bias is the tendency to look for, and focus on, evidence that confirms our prior beliefs, and ignore evidence that points in a different direction. This tendency is connected to our need to feel good about ourselves. Absorbing evidence that we’re wrong about something isn’t a reassuring or pleasant experience.
Suppose your new colleague is someone you knew as a kid. You haven’t seen him in years, but you remember he was always a jerk. Imagine that on his first day of work he makes a critical comment about a small error you’ve made in a report. You might take this as further evidence confirming that he is indeed a picky jerk. When he apologizes, you might well take this as evidence that he’s insincere and just trying to suck up to you—just what you would expect a jerk to do! In other words, you may have a tendency to interpret all new evidence in a way that’s consistent with what you already believed about your new colleague, rather than updating your beliefs based on that new evidence.
As business professionals trained in critical thinking, it’s particularly important to understand the significance of confirmation bias. Beliefs on complex topics, including many in the world of business, are best approached as tentative explanations we hold onto until we can disconfirm them. In fact, we should actively seek evidence that goes against our current beliefs in order to better test them. If we believe there’s a good market for recycled ping-pong balls, we should look for evidence against that belief. After all, it’s only by looking that we can be at least somewhat certain that such evidence isn’t out there! If we do look and find no such evidence, we’re then more reasonably justified in believing our initial hypothesis. Survivor Bias
Survivor bias is similar to confirmation bias in that it’s a tendency to ignore crucial evidence. This bias consists of taking into account only those people, things, or organizations that have reached a certain level of success, and ignoring those that haven’t. This can lead to seriously mistaken conclusions.
Perhaps the best illustration of the dangers of survivor bias comes from the web comic xkcd: A single panel depicts a motivational speaker on a stage, surrounded by bags of money, telling the audience, “Never stop buying lottery tickets, no matter what anyone tells you. I failed again and again, but I never gave up. I took extra jobs and poured the money into tickets. And here I am, proof that if you put in the time, it pays off!” Of course, the audience doesn’t get to hear from all the people who bought lottery tickets but never won. It’s a humorous illustration, but the point is quite serious. When somebody tells you, for instance, that you should follow a certain corporate strategy because “that’s how Google does it,” you need to be very careful. The fact that one very successful company follows a certain strategy is fine as far as it goes, but unless you also know how many unsuccessful companies have followed the same strategy, you can’t really tell whether the strategy is a good one.
This is a point to keep firmly in mind whenever someone successful promises to give you the keys to success by letting you know how they achieved theirs. Unless they have good evidence that there aren’t a bunch of people out there who tried the same things and failed, such promises are trying to take advantage of your survivor bias (and unfortunately, many books in the business section of any large bookstore fall into that category).
False Consensus
The false consensus effect is the tendency we have to overestimate the extent to which other people agree with us. When we need to exercise judgement, the false consensus effect can make us feel confident—more confident than we should be—that others share our point of view.
Imagine a CEO who, during a meeting with staff, proposes a particular way of financing a new business venture. Though no one at the table is enthusiastic about this approach, no one speaks up. The CEO walks away confident that the whole team likes the plan—after all, nobody spoke against it. This is the false consensus effect in action.
As a result of the false consensus effect, we can end up being more confident than we should be about the premises—the fundamental starting points—we use as the foundation of our arguments.

This is especially likely to be a problem in hierarchical organizations like corporations. Managers and others in positions of authority may find that others readily express agreement with them while keeping quiet when they disagree. This deprives executives of valuable sources of information and can easily lead to bad decisions.
Fighting Cognitive Biases
Unfortunately, there are few sure-fire ways to avoid falling prey to the cognitive biases discussed above. That’s because biases tend to operate when we don’t explicitly realize we’re even engaging in reasoning at all. If we don’t realize we’re reasoning, it’s hard to be on guard against mistakes in reasoning. We don’t set out, for example, to remember only evidence that supports our own previous conclusion—that is, to fall prey to confirmation bias. If we did, we’d likely recognize that such a strategy is problematic. But confirmation bias isn’t a strategy we choose—it just happens. Also, our best understanding of the psychology of cognitive biases suggests that they’re hardwired, built directly into the way our brains work. It’s therefore very difficult to “train” ourselves to resist cognitive biases; even people who’ve studied these biases for years find themselves following these problematic patterns of thinking.
But, while not as helpful as knowing about argumentative fallacies, knowing about cognitive biases doesn’t hurt either. At least knowing about these biases allows us to take a moment before making a big decision to think about whether any of the steps in our decision-making may have been the result of cognitive biases. However, we shouldn’t be too optimistic about this process; the problem here is that the psychological features that produce cognitive biases in the first place also make it difficult to thoroughly scan for biases in ourselves. Doing that takes time and effort – which we like to save on, and our tendency to overestimate our own abilities often extends to a sense that we’re less affected by cognitive biases than we really are.
Thus, the best way of putting our understanding of cognitive biases to use is to move the process of guarding against them out of our own brains by designing concrete policies, mechanisms, and procedures that will help correct for the negative effects such biases may have. At the individual level, we may try to create self-imposed rules, similar to how many people force themselves to exercise. To fight confirmation bias, for example, you might decide that you won’t allow yourself to make an important decision (say, a purchase over $500) before you’ve spent at least one hour searching the Internet for the best evidence that you’re about to make a mistake (note that the rule suggested here only concerns big decisions; seeking out evidence that contradicts your current beliefs is mentally draining, so it’s important to pick your battles so you don’t exhaust your capacities on issues that don’t really matter). Ideally, as with exercising, these self-imposed strategies can develop into habits, so you follow them without even thinking about them.

However, as with starting an exercise regime, actually following self-imposed rules designed to curb cognitive biases is easier when you enlist other people to monitor you. This brings us to an issue of particular importance in business contexts: guarding against the effects of cognitive biases not as an individual, but as a group, team, or organization. We’ll discuss this more in the reading about groupthink.
Conclusion
The cognitive biases discussed above constitute significant obstacles to clear thinking and effective decision-making; the fact that they are so common is evidence of how hard they are to overcome. Nonetheless, there are strategies that make it less likely that you and your organization will fall prey to them. In particular, focusing on instituting corrective policies, mechanisms, and procedures can go a long way to helping us fight against them.
A final word of warning: Perhaps you think that this is all true for most people, but that you yourself are perfectly capable of keeping your cognitive biases, if any, in check. In fact, you’re pretty likely to think that—because this kind of overconfidence is precisely what lies at the root of many of these biases in the first place. It’s your system 1 trying to maintain its supreme grasp on your reasoning and decision-making. By now we hope you know better than to fall for that.
