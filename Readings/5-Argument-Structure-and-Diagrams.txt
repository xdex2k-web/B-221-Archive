Argument Structure and Diagrams
Introduction
In the context of business decision-making, getting others to agree with you is crucial. This step is necessary as business is always a cooperative activity. You can’t conduct business by yourself—there’s always somebody you need to convince, whether it’s your boss, your team, your customers, etc. There are many ways you can (try to) convince others; some are more effective than others, and some are ethically problematic (such as brainwashing, deceiving, or bullying). In this course, we focus on what might be considered the gold standard of persuasion techniques: arguments. One major advantage of arguments over most other persuasion techniques is that they can be used not only to convince others when you’ve already made up your mind, but also to help you find the best answer to a question in cooperation with those to whom you present your arguments. In other words, arguments are useful in both stages of business decision-making: finding the best course of action, and convincing others of it. We’ll begin, this week, with argument analysis: a basic account of what an argument is, what its parts are, a simple visual method for understanding its ‘shape’.
What Is an Argument?
In everyday language, the word argument often refers to a dispute of some sort: a verbal debate or a heated exchange of opinions. An argument in that sense is something you have with someone. But the word argument also has a second, related meaning, which is what we’re interested in here. In this second sense, an argument is an attempt to convince someone of the truth of some claim or point of view. Thus understood, an argument is something you give someone else. It’s in this sense of the word that arguments are the most important tool of critical thinking. More formally, we can define the word argument this way:
Argument: A set of claims, some of which are intended to provide support for another of those claims.
Here’s a simple example:
Walter has very strong skills. He also works very hard. So, he’s definitely a valuable employee.
The terms claim and support in the definition above are very important. A claim is a statement that can be either true or false. A true claim is roughly the same as what we ordinarily refer to as a fact. We need to ask what support there is for that claim—what reasons there are to believe it. In other words, we need an argument. What an argument does is take one or more claims that are already accepted and use them as reasons for accepting another claim. This brings us to two further terms, used for the two main parts of any argument:
Premise: A claim used within an argument to support the argument’s conclusion.
Conclusion: A claim that constitutes the main point of an argument, the thing the arguer is trying to convince his or her audience of.
Every argument must have at least one premise and one conclusion. Without a conclusion, an argument literally has no point. And a “conclusion” without at least one premise—one reason to believe the conclusion—really wouldn’t be a conclusion at all, but rather just a bare claim, an undefended assertion or a mere opinion. (It’s important to point out, though, that in some cases an arguer will leave either the premise or conclusion unspoken. We’ll return to the issue of “missing” premises or conclusions below.) But generally, someone who’s trying to make an argument will at least tell you what their point (i.e., conclusion) is, and give you some reason to believe it (i.e., provide one or more premises).
Some arguments can, of course, be very long. There’s no limit, in principle, to how many premises an argument can have. Indeed, complex arguments can even have more than one conclusion, another point we will return to later in this chapter. So, while the examples used here are quite short, arguments can, in principle, be lengthy. Many non-fiction books, for example, are essentially long, complex arguments trying to convince the reader of some overarching conclusion.

Constructing and Reconstructing Arguments
One of the key challenges faced by anyone attempting to evaluate arguments is the flexibility of language. There are typically many ways to make a given point and to support it with reasons, and different people may express the same fundamental thought in very different ways. Considerations of style and efficiency can result in two different people making the same argument in different ways. That flexibility results in wonderful opportunities for creativity, but it also poses challenges to interpreting what exactly a person’s argument is.
In this chapter, we aim to develop the ability to clarify the structure of arguments. This is essential both in constructing our own arguments and in reconstructing the arguments of others. Most of us don’t always think in the most structured, logical way about the issues we want others to agree with us on. As a result, if we simply tell them what’s on our mind, we won’t produce a strong argument. We can discipline our thinking to produce a coherent argument, but this takes time and effort. But by practising the techniques introduced here, we can learn to cut down on the time and effort needed to structure our thoughts. Similarly, when we’re confronted with an argument made by somebody else, it usually doesn’t come out in the most structured, logical way either. They’ll say some things that are ambiguous or irrelevant, and will leave some relevant things out.
So, we need to learn to construct our own arguments and to reconstruct the arguments of others. The discussion in this chapter will mainly focus on argument reconstruction, as it’s simpler, in a book, to demonstrate the techniques by applying them to arguments encountered in writing. But the same techniques can be applied in constructing your own arguments. Indeed, one useful thing to do when constructing an argument is to write down a first draft and then reconstruct it in a way that makes it stronger, like you would with somebody else’s argument.

Argument reconstruction involves three basic tasks: identifying the claims in the argument, determining the status of each claim in the argument, and determining the relations between the claims in the argument. Roughly corresponding to these three tasks are three steps required in our diagramming language: numbering claims, underlining conclusions, and drawing a diagram. A diagram is a kind of technical drawing, in which we show the main parts of a thing in order to understand it better. Think of how useful human anatomical diagrams are in learning about anatomy, for instance. It’s crucial to see that, in diagramming an argument, we’re not attempting to include every aspect of the argument. Any diagram (of anything) is a simplification, and diagrams of arguments are no different. This chapter will guide you through the process of argument reconstruction, while simultaneously introducing our method for diagramming arguments.
Identifying (and Numbering) the Claims in an Argument
The first step in reconstructing an argument is to make sure you understand everything that’s been claimed. This can involve considerable challenges, especially when the argument is delivered in spoken form—you might not be able to hear the speaker very well, or they might be talking too fast for you to remember or write down everything they said. Speakers might be prone to tangents or have unclear diction, transmission equipment may be faulty (in the case of remote conversations), or the argument may be presented in a language you aren’t entirely fluent in. But let’s set these concerns aside and focus instead on cases where the argument is presented in reasonably well-written English. In such cases, identifying the claims made in the argument is a fairly straightforward task. But doing so is an essential step in reconstructing the argument, and there are a couple of potential hiccups.
Here’s one of our examples from above:

This second example demonstrates a potential problem in identifying claims: it’s not the case that every sentence in English (or any other language) contains exactly one claim. It’s quite common for sentences to contain more than one claim, or to contain no (complete) claims at all. And, importantly, not every claim that appears in an author’s argument is something that the author actually asserts (or claims): in other words, some claims are embedded. Imagine Mario saying, “Ryba says Jim’s car was stolen. But she is a pathological liar.” Now, Mario has uttered the claim Jim’s car was stolen, but he clearly doesn’t mean to assert this claim. What he really claims isn’t about Jim’s car, but about what Ryba said. If Jim’s car hasn’t been stolen, it would be unfair to call Mario a liar (Ryba is the liar in that case). By contrast, if it turns out that Ryba never said anything about Jim’s car, that would make Mario a liar. Relaying claims made by somebody else is one important category of claim that an arguer may make without asserting that claim themself.
Another such category appears in conditional statements. For example, imagine Mario saying, “If Italy wins the World Cup this year, I’ll win a million dollars.” There are two claims in this statement—one about Italy winning the World Cup, and one about Mario winning a million dollars—but Mario isn’t asserting either of them. What he’s asserting is something about the relationship between these two claims—specifically, that if the first is true, then so is the second (Mario’s claim is a conditional claim, a type of claim we will discuss in more detail in chapter 10). Again, what he said would not be false if Italy didn’t win the World Cup, or if he didn’t win a million dollars. The reason we’re harping on this here is that when numbering claims in an argument, you should only number claims that the arguer themself means to assert. So, suppose Mario makes the following argument:
Argument D:
The betting-odds on an Italy World Cup win on BetBoys.sx are 10:1. I’ve placed a $100,000 bet with them. So, if Italy wins the World Cup this year, I’ll win a million dollars.

Determining the Status of Claims (and Underlining Conclusions)
Once we’ve identified the claims advanced in an argument, it’s time to figure out which claims are playing which role—that is, which are premises and which are conclusions. With short arguments, we’ll often be able to immediately see which role each claim plays. When it comes to longer, more complex arguments, one good rule of thumb is to begin by figuring out what the argument’s main conclusion is. Once we identify an argument’s main point, we can go on to figure out what reasons—premises—are being offered in support of that point, and we can also begin to consider whether, for example, we need to fill in any missing premises.
One way we can begin to figure out the status of claims in an argument is to look for what are called indicator words. The English language makes use of a number of different words that help listeners understand the flow of an arguer’s logic, by indicating whether a particular claim is being put forward as a premise or as a conclusion. The word therefore is a good example. Whenever you hear the word therefore, you know the speaker is about to arrive at a conclusion. We’ll refer to words like therefore as conclusion indicators.
There are literally dozens of conclusion indicators in the English language. Some of them are single words (like therefore or so) and some are short phrases (like and so we may conclude that). Here are just a handful of common conclusion indicators:

Once we’ve identified the claims in an argument and determined their status, we’re in a position to figure out whether any essential claims are missing from the argument. Discussing this will also give us the opportunity to introduce two important principles of argument reconstruction.
Missing Parts and Hidden Assumptions
Why do we sometimes read or hear arguments that seem to be missing premises or missing a conclusion? Why do people leave pieces of their argument unstated? There are many reasons. In some cases, the arguer is simply trying to be brief, and leaves out something that she or he assumes the audience will understand as taken for granted. And in many contexts, that assumption is entirely fair. Indeed, it’s often the case that stating all the parts of an argument that are commonly accepted would be laborious. (Imagine a manager who, every time she explains a new strategy to her team, rehearses everyone’s roles and responsibilities, as well as reminding everybody that the company is trying to turn a profit, et cetera.) In making arguments, as in all other parts of life, we’re relying on myriad assumptions about how we know the world works. There’s no need to mention all of them.
So, there isn’t necessarily anything wrong with leaving something out of your argument. But in other cases, the omission happens by mistake, as when an arguer thinks he or she has provided a full set of reasons in support of some conclusion, but fails to recognize that an important piece of the argument is missing. In still other cases, an arguer may leave part of her or his argument unstated for less noble reasons; perhaps he or she knows that the audience will find one particular premise unacceptable, so leaving it unstated may make it easier to gain the audience’s approval. Or the person relies on a premise that, though accepted by one part of their audience, isn’t something they want to be held accountable for by the public (think of a politician who makes arguments that rely on premises about the inferiority of some subset of the population).

The easiest way of demonstrating the principles of argument reconstruction is to apply them to the problem of missing argument parts. Steffi: Do you think the Annual Report is ready to be distributed?

Missing premises and conclusions are perfectly natural elements of language use. People leave out parts of their arguments, for good reasons and bad, all the time, and we still generally understand their meaning (see the textbox titled Missing Parts and Hidden Assumptions). But in order to reconstruct and evaluate an argument you need to be aware of all of its parts, even the ones the arguer has for some reason left unstated. When faced with the need to evaluate someone’s argument, it’s your job as evaluator to try to determine what’s been left unstated and assess the acceptability of the unstated parts.
Supplying the missing pieces of an argument is as challenging as it is important. But it’s not always clear what the missing pieces are. Sometimes, people put forward arguments that simply aren’t clear, either because they’ve used words in a way that’s unclear or because the thinking behind those words is itself muddy. And in some cases, the arguer gives us very little to go on—perhaps just a small fragment suggesting that there’s an argument—without clarifying what they really have in mind. Figuring out an argument’s meaning in such cases can be a challenge.
So how do we fill in the missing pieces? There’s no straightforward formula for doing so. The key is to remember that, when filling in missing pieces, we are effectively “putting words into someone’s mouth,” so we need to be careful to do so in a way that is accurate and fair. This is why, in supplying missing pieces, we should follow the two principles of argument reconstruction:
The principle of fidelity: First, we want to reconstruct the argument (by supplying missing pieces) in a way that’s faithful to the arguer’s intentions. We should try, to the best of our ability, to stick to what it is that the arguer is actually trying to argue.
The principle of charity: If in doubt about an arguer’s meaning, we should reconstruct the argument (by supplying missing pieces) in a way that makes the arguer look smart, rather than foolish. If there is doubt, for example, as to which of two things an arguer might mean, we should assume that he or she means whichever thing would make the argument more reasonable.

If we’re sincere in wanting to understand and evaluate a particular argument, then we must do our best to focus on the argument as it actually is, rather than on a distorted version of that argument. In the case at hand, the best way to fill in the argument is probably to assume that John’s missing premise is Tina will accept the promotion.

Diagramming an Argument
Once we’ve assigned numbers to each claim in an argument (including missing claims), and underlined intermediate and overall conclusions, we’re ready to draw a diagram of the argument.
In our diagramming language, we’ll represent arguments by creating flow charts that flow down the page towards the conclusion. Each premise in our argument will be represented by a square, with the number associated with that premise written inside. The conclusion of our argument will be represented by a circle, again with its associated number written inside. We will use arrows to show the flow of logic from premises to conclusion.

Independent premises: premises that the author intends to work separately in support of the conclusion.

Figuring out whether premises are joint or independent is an important part of reconstructing an argument. In doing so, we need to apply the principles of fidelity and charity. Sometimes, the arguer will give us a hint (for example, they may signal an independent premise by saying something like an unrelated reason…), in which case the principle of fidelity dictates that we stick with the arguer’s intention. But often, there are no such clues, in which case we apply the principle of charity, and figure out whether the premises make most sense conceived as independent or joint premises. There’s a simple test we can apply here (that we’ve already implicitly used in the last paragraph). Here’s how it works: Assume that the opposite of one of the premises is true, then ask yourself whether the other premise still provides any reasonable support for the conclusion. If the answer is no, you’re dealing with joint premises; if the answer is yes, the premises are independent.
You can see how this worked for argument I: once we assume that the CEO has signed off on the annual report, the fact that her signature is required provides no reason to think the report can’t be sent out. And, conversely, once we assume the signature isn’t required, the fact that she has not signed provides, again, no reason to think we shouldn’t send the report out. Contrast this with argument H: Even if we assume the government has no budget issues, the absence of any expression of concern about a product is still reason to think no regulations will be forthcoming; conversely, even if they did express concern, the budget freeze would still provide some reason to think they won’t regulate. Thus, these premises operate independently. Now, this doesn’t mean that each premise by itself provides sufficient support for the conclusion—maybe they do, maybe they don’t. The point, rather, is that whatever support they do provide (weak or strong) is not dependent on the acceptability of another premise.1

Strong and Weak Arguments
We’ve now arrived at an all-important question. What makes a good argument? A good argument is one in which the premises provide sufficient support for the conclusion. To see what this means, it’s easiest to start by noting that an argument can be weak in just two key ways:
1) It can have premises that aren’t acceptable.
2) It can use poor logic.
Exploring each of these problems in some depth is the task of later chapters. Here, we simply want to stress that these two are quite separate issues. An argument can have good premises (claims that are acceptable) but those premises might not do much to support the conclusion being offered. Consider this argument:
Argument K:
Premise 1: Toronto is in Ontario.
Premise 2: Timmins is in Ontario.
Conclusion: Therefore, all cities in Ontario start with T.
Both premises of this argument are acceptable: they’re easily verified. But do they logically support the conclusion? They’re both probably relevant: Toronto and Timmins are both cities starting with T, so they belong to the class of things referred to in the conclusion. So they’re on topic. But of course, the logic of this argument is deeply flawed. Those two premises, despite being acceptable and relevant, do very little to support such a grand conclusion. This means, among other things, that we can’t establish whether an argument is strong or weak simply by looking at the argument’s premises. The acceptability of an argument’s premises is typically a good place to start when evaluating an argument, but it isn’t the only thing you need to look at. You also need to look at the flow of logic from premises to conclusion. And this is part of why diagramming arguments is so useful. A diagram demonstrates just how many premises there are—how many premises need to be evaluated for acceptability—and how many flows of logic must be assessed.
